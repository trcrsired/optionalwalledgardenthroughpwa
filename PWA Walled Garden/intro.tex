\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

For most of computing history, users enjoyed broad freedom to install, modify, and repair software and hardware on their own devices. This freedom is closely tied to the \textit{right to repair} and the traditional understanding that personal property belongs to its owner. With the rise of the Internet in the 1980s and 1990s, however, this freedom increasingly collided with security risks. Non‑technical users could easily download malware\cite{microsoftPreventMalware}, fall for phishing\cite{microsoftPhishingTrends}, or compromise entire systems. Nearly all Windows users now rely on antivirus software, and the 2024 CrowdStrike incident demonstrated the fragility of this model: a single signed update caused global blue screens and disrupted critical infrastructure\cite{wikipedia2024crowdstrike}.

The introduction of the iPhone in 2007 accelerated a shift toward \textit{walled gardens}. Users could no longer run arbitrary binaries, install alternative operating systems, or bypass app stores\cite{apple2021trusted, melontini2025bootloader}. Apple and Google now control app distribution and take a 30\% cut\cite{FreeBSDfan2024, haney2023users}. Stallman and the FSF describe this as \texttt{tivoization}\cite{GNUtivoization}. Governments have responded with antitrust actions\cite{usdoj2024apple,europa2024apple,samr2025google}, yet both app stores still host large numbers of scam and predatory applications\cite{10.1145/3548606.3560615, 10.1145/2484313.2484316}.

These restrictions extend far beyond app stores. Companies increasingly deploy software locks, cryptographic pairing, and proprietary components to undermine repair rights. The DMCA is routinely used to block legitimate repair and research\cite{greengardfightingrighttorepair2025}. Groups such as Keep Android Open\cite{keepandroidopen} document how Android devices are becoming more locked down despite being marketed as "open." Regulators such as the FTC and FDA have found "scant evidence" supporting Big Tech’s claims that these restrictions are necessary for safety; they primarily preserve ecosystem control\cite{nixingthefix}.

Recent events highlight this fragility. Google's 2025 developer verification rollout broke legitimate apps and locked out independent developers, illustrating how a single platform decision can destabilize the entire Android ecosystem. This is part of a broader pattern of \textit{enshittification}, where platforms degrade over time to maximize control and revenue.

These practices also worsen the global e‑waste crisis. A 2023 PIRG report found that U.S. households spend \$1,767 annually on electronics—a 19\% increase since 2019—and could save \$49.6 billion per year with better repair access. The WHO reports that only 22.3\% of global e‑waste is formally recycled. A CBS News investigation\cite{cbsEwasteTragicCosts} documented how more than 50 million tons of e‑waste are produced annually, most of which is exported to the global South, where informal recyclers sift through toxic debris without protection. Researchers have found heavy‑metal contamination even in the breast milk of mothers in affected African communities. In China, independent estimates suggest the real recycling rate is below 8\%, with entire villages suffering cancer clusters due to informal e‑waste processing. Locked‑down, non‑repairable devices directly fuel this environmental harm.

A parallel trend accelerating the erosion of digital autonomy is the industry's push toward cloud dependence. As the saying goes, ``the cloud is just someone else's computer,``\cite{win11mustbestopped} yet major vendors increasingly design products that require remote servers for basic functionality. The fragility of this model became clear on March 1, 2026, when an Amazon Web Services data center in the United Arab Emirates was struck by unidentified objects during a period of intensified regional conflict involving Iran, the United States, and Israel. The impact triggered sparks and a fire, leading emergency crews to cut power and causing outages across multiple availability zones. AWS did not confirm whether the incident was directly linked to Iranian missile and drone strikes, but the outage occurred amid broader retaliatory attacks across the Gulf region. This event underscored how cloud dependence centralizes risk: a single facility failure can disrupt critical services across entire regions.

This centralization reflects a broader shift toward subscription models, remote execution, and platform‑controlled ecosystems. The World Economic Forum’s 2016 scenario essay, which popularized the phrase ``You will own nothing and you will be happy,``\cite{ownnothingbehappy} has become a widely cited symbol of public concern that personal ownership is being replaced by perpetual rental. The trend is visible across modern computing: devices require cloud authentication, applications depend on vendor accounts, and essential functions run on remote servers rather than locally. Scholars describe this structure as a form of techno‑feudalism, in which users rely on platform landlords who control access, updates, and even continued device operation. Combined with the physical centralization of cloud infrastructure—where a single data‑center outage can disrupt entire regions—this shift erodes the autonomy and resilience that once defined personal computing.

Even the supposed ``security'' benefits of locked ecosystems are overstated. Signed binaries still fail: the CrowdStrike update was signed, kernel anti‑cheat drivers are abused for surveillance, and projects like Notepad++ reject mandatory signing due to centralization risks\cite{notepadSelfSigned}. Rising hardware prices further erode consumer ownership.

At this point, it becomes clear that \textbf{security is only the surface cause}. The \textbf{root cause is the erosion of personal ownership}. As Perzanowski and Schultz argue in \textit{The End of Ownership}\cite{10.7551/mitpress/9780262035019.001.0001}, intellectual property law has expanded beyond its intended scope and now routinely overrides traditional personal property rights. Buying two identical devices does not grant the right to repair one with parts from the other; companies claim that software licenses supersede ownership. This inversion of property rights is a central driver of enshittification across the tech industry.

These dynamics have long‑term consequences. Windows cannot adopt the same restrictions as mobile platforms because developers do not target the Microsoft Store at comparable scale. Windows S mode\cite{MicrosoftSModeFAQ} lacks apps, and Windows users strongly prefer open software distribution\cite{pcgamerUniversalApps, redditRufusUWP}. Meanwhile, many Android devices stop receiving updates after only a few years\cite{androidcentralLinuxKernel}, leaving outdated kernels and unpatched vulnerabilities\cite{androidvulnerabilitiesByYear}.

The common narrative that users must trade freedom for security is itself misleading. Even the surface explanation---that locked-down ecosystems are necessary for safety---does not withstand scrutiny. As shown throughout this section, these restrictions routinely fail to deliver meaningful security benefits, while simultaneously blocking repair, modification, and user autonomy. The deeper issue is not security at all, but the steady dismantling of personal ownership in favor of corporate control. Users have been losing authority over their hardware, software, and repair rights\cite{win11stop}, not because this loss makes devices safer, but because it strengthens the power of platform gatekeepers. This paper argues that the supposed tension between security and usability is a false dilemma\cite{nistUsersNotStupid}: we can design systems that provide strong protection without sacrificing ownership, openness, or user freedom.

With the rise of Progressive Web Apps (PWAs)\cite{8399228, webDevPWA}, many services—including Office, YouTube, TikTok, Spotify, Instagram, Discord, Reddit, LinkedIn, VSCode, Copilot, ChatGPT, and more—now offer PWA versions. PWAs run inside browser sandboxes, reducing risk compared to native Win32 binaries. This makes it possible to design an \textit{optional walled‑garden mode} for non‑technical users while preserving full openness for power users.

The S/P mode proposed in this paper aims to show that the supposed trade‑off between security and freedom is false. Users can have both: a safe, simplified environment for everyday use and a fully open, repair‑friendly, developer‑friendly environment for those who need it—without surrendering ownership or falling into enshittified ecosystems.
